1, 2, and 3

To start, let's begin with some basic concepts. Firstness, Secondness, and Thirdness are the three fundamental categories in Peirce’s philosophy. Firstness is the realm of pure possibility, feeling, and quality—it’s the raw, unanalyzed sensation of something, like the redness of a rose or the feeling of joy. Secondness is the realm of actuality, reaction, and brute force — it’s the experience of something pushing back against you, like the resistance of a door when you try to open it. Thirdness is the realm of law and meaning. It’s the process of connecting things, like understanding that a red light means “stop.” These three categories are the building blocks of Peirce’s semiotics and metaphysical theories.

Building upon these foundational categories, it's crucial to understand that Firstness, Secondness, and Thirdness are not mutually exclusive, isolated boxes. Instead, they are interwoven and interdependent, forming a dynamic and constantly evolving system. While Firstness might exist in potentia, it only gains significance through its interaction with Secondness, the realm of actual experience. And it is through Thirdness that the relationship between the potential (Firstness) and the actual (Secondness) is mediated, generating meaning, habits, and ultimately, knowledge. This triadic relationship is not static; it's a continuous process of interpretation and growth, a framework that Peirce believed applies to everything from the simplest sensations to the most complex scientific theories.
